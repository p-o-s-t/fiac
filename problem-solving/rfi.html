The Request for Information (RFI) Workflow

A Request for Information (RFI) is a formal process triggered when an analyst, defender, or decision-maker identifies a critical gap in their active threat intelligence picture.

Unlike persistent, strategic PIRs, RFIs are often ad-hoc, tactical, or operational requests that arise during incidents or specific security evaluations to support rapid decision-making.

Anatomy of a Well-Structured RFI

To receive a high-quality response, the requestor must formulate a well-structured query. A robust RFI includes the following components:

  • Context: Background explanation detailing *why* the information is being requested.
  • Specific Questions: Precise, unambiguous, and numbered queries that avoid open-ended phrasing.
  • Operational Constraints: The format required (e.g., raw CSV, brief email, PDF report) and target audience.
  • Timelines & TLP: A clear, justified deadline and the Traffic Light Protocol (TLP) sharing classification.

Formulating a Search & Collection Strategy

When an RFI is assigned, the analyst first builds a collection strategy to gather relevant evidence from structured and unstructured repositories:

  • Internal Databases: Querying the organization's SIEM logs, EDR telemetry, incident tickets, and threat intelligence platform (TIP).
  • External Repositories: Searching OSINT feeds, malware sharing hubs (e.g., VirusTotal), and vulnerability databases.
  • Industry Contacts: Engaging trust groups (ISACs) or vetted intelligence sharing partners to gather peer telemetry.

Drafting the RFI Response

Formatting the final answer is as important as the analysis itself. An effective RFI response adheres to these presentation rules:

  • BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front): Place the direct answers to the RFI's core questions at the very top of the response.
  • Analytical Objectivity: Distinguish clearly between raw facts, analytical assessments, and assumptions.
  • Uncertainty & Estimative Language: Express analytical confidence levels (e.g., "highly likely," "possible") based on source quality.
  • Rigorous Sourcing: Provide complete citations and references for every claim to establish trust and auditing capability.

The RFI Lifecycle & Metric Tracking

The RFI process is not complete when the answer is delivered. Effective threat intelligence teams manage the closure and metrics phases:

  • Database Archiving: Logging the RFI and answer in a central system so future analysts can search and reuse the findings.
  • Metric Tracking: Measuring performance statistics like response time (SLA compliance), accuracy, and stakeholder satisfaction.
  • Detection Enrichment: Translating the newly acquired intelligence into EDR detections, firewall blocks, or SIEM watchlists.
