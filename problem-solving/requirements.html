Demystifying Intelligence Requirements

Intelligence does not begin with data collection; it begins with a question. An Intelligence Requirement (IR) is a formal statement of an information need that a decision-maker must resolve to reduce uncertainty.

Without clear, structured requirements, threat intelligence teams suffer from "indicator fatigue," collecting data aimlessly without delivering actionable business value.

Priority Intelligence Requirements (PIRs)

PIRs represent the most critical, high-level threat-related questions facing an organization. They are directly aligned with business risks and strategic decisions.

  • Stakeholder-Driven: Created in collaboration with executives, risk officers, and security leadership.
  • Strategic Focus: Focus on adversary campaigns, capabilities, and motivations targeting the organization's sector or region.
  • Example: "What threat actors are active in our geographic region with the capability and intent to disrupt our cloud infrastructure?"

Specific Intelligence Requirements (SIRs)

SIRs translate high-level, strategic PIRs into actionable, operational questions that the security operations and threat intelligence teams can address.

  • Tactical Breakdown: Sub-questions derived from the parent PIR that target specific technical mechanisms.
  • Collector Alignment: Grouped by target systems, technologies, or specific threat groups.
  • Example: "What command-and-control (C2) protocols are utilized by the APT groups targeting our cloud environment, and how do they establish persistence?"

Indicators of Information (IoIs)

At the bottom of the requirement hierarchy are Indicators of Information (IoIs) (sometimes called Specific Information Requirements). These are the specific, atomic data points sought to answer SIRs.

  • Technical Queries: File hashes, domain names, configuration artifacts, EDR registry keys, and vulnerability CVE IDs.
  • Validation: The tangible evidence that is gathered during the collection phase.
  • Example: "Look for DNS requests to dynamic DNS domains using non-standard ports or specific user agent strings linked to APT29."

Designing Effective PIRs

Not all questions make good PIRs. When designing intelligence requirements, analysts follow strict structural guidelines:

  • Singular Focus: Each PIR should address only one key threat or question.
  • Neutrality: Avoid leading questions or embedded assumptions (e.g., ask "How likely..." rather than "When will...").
  • Clear and Answerable: Must specify who, what, where, and when, and be answerable with available or obtainable data.
  • Decision-Aligned: Must be linked to a concrete action the organization can take once the question is answered.
