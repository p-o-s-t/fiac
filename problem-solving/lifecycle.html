The Intelligence Process

Intelligence is not a static repository of facts; it is a continuous, cyclic process. It starts with an analytical need and culminates in a finished product that directly drives the next phase of questioning.

Commonly referred to as the Intelligence Lifecycle, this methodology ensures that intelligence remains relevant, timely, objective, and action-oriented for stakeholders.

1. Planning and Direction

This foundational phase defines the scope of the intelligence effort by identifying core information requirements driven by stakeholders.

  • Priority Intelligence Requirements (PIRs): High-level threat-related questions defined by decision-makers to steer resources.
  • Scoping & Constraints: Defining the timeline, boundaries, and classification restrictions of the target requirement.
  • Focus: Prevents "intelligence for the sake of intelligence" by keeping resources strictly aligned with operational risks.

2. Collection

Collection involves gathering raw data from a diverse, hybrid set of sources, including external intelligence feeds and internal security systems.

  • Internal Telemetry: EDR logs, firewall events, SIEM alerts, and security incidents.
  • OSINT (Open Source Intelligence): Security blogs, social media (e.g., threat researcher accounts), GitHub repositories, and CVE databases.
  • Commercial & Closed Feeds: Premium threat feeds, dark web monitoring, and industry-sharing groups (ISACs).

3. Processing & Exploitation

Raw data collected in the previous step is rarely immediately ready for analysis. This phase transforms it into a structured, usable format.

  • Normalization & Parsing: Converting disparate log formats (JSON, XML, Syslog) into a standard schema.
  • Decryption & Decoding: Reversing adversary obfuscation (e.g., Base64 decoding, decrypting malicious payloads).
  • Translation & Structuring: Translating foreign language actor reports and indexing indicators into databases (like MISP).

4. Analysis & Production

This is the core cognitive phase where structured analysis turns processed data into actual intelligence by applying logical reasoning.

  • Structured Analytic Techniques (SATs): Using formal methods (e.g., Analysis of Competing Hypotheses) to minimize cognitive bias.
  • Contextualization: Linking raw IOCs to known adversary TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures).
  • Assessment & Estimation: Drawing conclusions regarding actor intent, capability, and likely future actions using estimative language.

5. Dissemination & Feedback

The final phase delivers the finished intelligence products to the stakeholders and collects feedback to refine the next lifecycle iteration.

  • Strategic Deliverables: Executive summaries and threat briefs focusing on business impact for leadership.
  • Operational/Technical Deliverables: Detailed indicators, detection rules (YARA/Sigma), and EDR queries for security operations.
  • The Feedback Loop: Reviewing the utility of the product with stakeholders to adjust future requirements and update active PIRs.
