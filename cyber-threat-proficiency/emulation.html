What is Adversary Emulation?

Adversary Emulation is a type of security testing where defenders mimic the known TTPs of a specific threat actor to verify how well their organization can detect and respond to those specific behaviors.

Unlike traditional red teaming, which is goal-oriented (e.g., "get the domain admin"), emulation is behavior-oriented.

Emulation vs. Simulation

While often used interchangeably, there is a subtle difference in a CTI context:

  • Simulation: Creating a custom or synthetic "threat-like" behavior to test a control.
  • Emulation: Following a specific "recipe" or playbook derived directly from CTI reports about a real-world actor (e.g., "Emulate APT29's use of ISO files for initial access").

Tools and Frameworks

Adversary emulation is often performed using specialized tools mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework:

  • Atomic Red Team: A library of simple, modular tests that every security team can execute to test their defenses.
  • CALDERA: An automated adversary emulation system that allows for the creation and execution of complex operation chains.
  • MITRE Emulation Plans: Publicly available, step-by-step guides for emulating sophisticated actors like FIN6 or APT29.

Designing an Emulation Campaign

A systematic process ensures that adversary emulation runs safely and effectively:

  • Threat Selection: Choosing an adversary relevant to the organization's industry, geographic location, and intelligence profile.
  • TTP Extraction: Analyzing intelligence reports to extract specific techniques and mapping them to MITRE ATT&CK.
  • Scope & Safety Boundaries: Defining rules of engagement, target endpoints, and testing schedules to prevent disruption.
  • Execution & Logging: Conducting the operations while confirming that host, network, and security logs are recording the activity.

Purple Teaming Integration

Emulation serves as the bridge between red and blue teams in a Purple Team engagement:

  • Collaborative Analysis: Red and blue teams review the timing and output of emulation runs together in real time.
  • Detection Gap Identification: Confirming whether the security stack (EDR, SIEM, firewall) blocked, alerted, or missed each action.
  • Signature & Rule Creation: Drafting and deploying detection rules (e.g., Sigma, Yara) to address the identified gaps.
  • Continuous Validation: Re-executing tests to verify that modifications to security configurations successfully catch the threat.
