Introduction to Cyber Threat Intelligence

Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) is the evidence-based knowledge—including context, mechanisms, indicators, implications, and actionable advice—about an existing or emerging hazard to assets.

CTI is not simply raw logs or threat feeds. It uses the DIKW Pyramid (Data, Information, Knowledge, Wisdom) to transform raw data points into actionable security wisdom.

Core Objectives of CTI

CTI operations seek to reduce the uncertainty surrounding threat events, allowing security leaders to allocate resources efficiently.

  • Proactive Defense: Enabling organizations to deploy defenses and verify controls before an attacker strikes.
  • Strategic Risk Reduction: Guiding budgeting, threat modeling, and long-term security architecture planning.
  • Incident Response Acceleration: Providing context (e.g., actor playbook, tools) during active breaches to speed up containment.

The Spectrum: Strategic & Operational

Intelligence is divided into tiers based on its target audience, scope, and lifecycle duration:

  • Strategic Intelligence: High-level threat landscapes, attribution, and geopolitical motives. Designed for executives (CISOs, board members) to guide risk management and capital investments.
  • Operational Intelligence: Details of ongoing or incoming campaigns, threat group movements, and capabilities. Designed for security managers to prioritize resources.

The Spectrum: Tactical & Technical

Lower-tier intelligence focuses on immediate technical actions and engineering-level defense mechanisms:

  • Tactical Intelligence: Focuses on adversary Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs), mapping attacker behaviors (like credential dumping). Designed for SOC analysts and detection engineers.
  • Technical Intelligence: Short-lived, atomic indicators of compromise (IoCs) like malicious IPs, file hashes, and registry values used directly in EDR or SIEM watchlists.

Consumers of CTI

CTI must be tailored to meet the varying requirements of different teams across the enterprise:

  • Security Operations (SOC): Uses technical indicators for alerting and tactical profiles to triage alerts.
  • Vulnerability Management: Integrates exploit threat data (e.g., EPSS scores) to prioritize patch cycles.
  • Executive Leadership (C-Suite): Consumes strategic briefings to evaluate business risk and align corporate policy with threat realities.
