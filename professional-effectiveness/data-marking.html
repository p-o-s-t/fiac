Information Security & Sharing

Threat intelligence sharing is vital to collective defense. However, CTI reports often contain sensitive details about an organization’s proprietary systems, security posture, and active investigations.

To facilitate secure exchange, analysts use standardized data marking protocols that establish legal boundaries for who can view the information and what actions they can perform with it.

Traffic Light Protocol (TLP 2.0)

Created by FIRST, the Traffic Light Protocol (TLP) is the industry standard for indicating the sharing restrictions of a threat intelligence product:

  • TLP:RED: Restricted to the specific human recipients. No further sharing inside or outside the organization.
  • TLP:AMBER: Shared within the recipient's organization and its immediate clients on a strict "need-to-know" basis.
  • TLP:AMBER+STRICT: Restricts sharing strictly to the recipient's organization (clients are excluded).
  • TLP:GREEN: Shared within the wider peer community, trust groups (ISACs), or sector partners. Not for public disclosure.
  • TLP:CLEAR: Publicly releasable. No sharing restrictions (replaces the older TLP:WHITE label).

Permissible Actions Protocol (PAP)

While TLP dictates *who* has access to data, the Permissible Actions Protocol (PAP) specifies *what actions* the recipient can take once they have the data:

  • PAP:RED: Non-detectable operations only. Do not use this data for active EDR blocks, firewall rules, or DNS blocking, as this could alert the attacker.
  • PAP:AMBER: Limited action. Detections can be configured, but only within the internal organization's infrastructure.
  • PAP:GREEN: Active defensive operations allowed. The indicators can be used for automated defenses and shared with trusted service providers.
  • PAP:WHITE: No restrictions. Actions can be taken publicly, including active scans or scanning remote infrastructure.

Operational Safeguards & Trusts

Beyond TLP and PAP, analysts navigate other trust structures when sharing threat intelligence details:

  • Chatham House Rule: Allows recipients to use the information received, but prohibits identifying the identity of the speaker or their organization.
  • Source Anonymization: Scrubbing local IP subnets, user names, and domain tags from reports before sharing them with public or community platforms.
  • NDAs & Bilateral Agreements: Establishing formal non-disclosure agreements before initiating direct exchanges of raw malware or breach artifacts.

Applying Labels at Scale

Data marking must be automated to remain effective across high-volume sharing environments:

  • Header & Footer Tagging: Standardized text labels placed at the top and bottom of every page in threat advisory PDFs and emails.
  • STIX 2.1 Metadata: Encoding sharing policies directly into threat indicators using the STIX marking-definition object.
  • TIP Automations: Configuring threat intelligence platforms (like MISP) to enforce TLP tags during automated TAXII feed synchronization.
