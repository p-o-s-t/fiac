Principles of CTI Reporting

Intelligence is only as valuable as the decisions it enables. An analyst may perform outstanding technical research, but if the final deliverable fails to communicate the threat clearly, the work is functionally useless.

CTI reporting follows strict professional guidelines: it must be structured, clear, precise, and immediately highlight the operational impact for the target stakeholder.

Implementing BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)

A core practice in intelligence reporting is placing your most critical assessments and recommendations at the very beginning of the document.

  • The Executive Summary: The first section of the report must state what has happened, why it matters, and what action is required.
  • Context-First: Avoid forcing busy readers to read through five pages of technical analysis before reaching the conclusion.
  • Example: *“A zero-day exploit targeting our mail server is actively being used in our sector. Action is required immediately to isolate these servers.”*

Estimative Language & Analytic Rigor

Threat intelligence reports deal with incomplete information. To maintain credibility and precision, analysts align with ODNI ICD 203 Standards:

  • Confidence Levels: Explicitly state analytical confidence: *High Confidence* (solid sources, logical patterns), *Medium Confidence* (interpretable data but gaps exist), or *Low Confidence* (fragmented or uncorroborated sources).
  • Probability Expressions: Use standardized words to communicate likelihood: *Almost Certain* (95%+), *Highly Likely* (70%-90%), *Likely* (55%-69%), *Unlikely* (20%-45%).
  • Separating Fact from Assessment: Always make it clear where raw evidence ends and analytical interpretation begins.

Structured Reporting Formats

Intelligence teams use standardized formats depending on the nature of the findings and the delivery mechanism:

  • Threat Advisories: Fast-paced, tactical documents focusing on a single malware campaign, detailing atomic IoCs and host signatures (YARA).
  • Threat Activity Briefs: Operational reports outlining threat actor movements, campaigns, and shifts in regional activity.
  • Strategic Assessments: Long-term reports looking at industry threat trends, threat models, and predictions for the upcoming quarter.

Targeting Your Audience

A single report cannot serve all stakeholders. Analysts customize report complexity and depth based on the consumer's role:

  • Executive Reports: Focus on business impact, cost of downtime, resource impact, and mitigation requirements. Tech details are omitted or placed in appendices.
  • Defender Advisories: Packed with registry paths, system files, network signatures, EDR queries, and step-by-step mitigation instructions for SOC and IR teams.
